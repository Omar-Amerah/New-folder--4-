    Power Infrastructure reference

    Cable tiers. Light Cable (low capacity) suits short low-demand final branches. Standard Cable (medium capacity) is the normal general-purpose distribution tier. Heavy Bus (high capacity) is worth its cost only on major high-throughput trunks. Heavy cable placed after a Light bottleneck does not remove that bottleneck — capacity is limited by the weakest section on the route.

    Sustained vs peak. Below sustained capacity a cable runs continuously. Between sustained and peak it works, but generates extra cable Heat and accumulates overload stress over time; delivery is always capped at peak. Physical cables never trip, burn or take damage from overload — when their load falls, stress recovers.

    Switchgear. An optional two-terminal component (not required for ordinary branches). Saved modes: Open isolates, Closed always connects through its rating, Automatic closes only for a safe, useful spare-power transfer that never sacrifices donor-side or equal-priority demand. A conducting Switchgear that stays overloaded trips (runtime Tripped state), waits out a cooldown, then deterministically retries — a safe retry restores it, an unsafe one stays Tripped and retries later. A Destroyed Switchgear stops conducting until repaired, then returns to its saved mode.

    Priorities. When Power is short, delivery follows your saved priority order across the six categories: command, propulsion, shields, point defence, weapons, cooling & support. Shields and point defence are always separate categories.

    Architectures. Redundancy improves resilience but always costs money and Heat capacity.

    Data wiring is separate. Data cables are a single cheap tier with their own cost/displacement and no capacity, overload, Heat or breaker mechanics.
